## Abstract

Agricultural robots such as STEMbot seek to enable autonomous navigation of dense plant canopies. For motion planning, STEMbot must be able to reason about plant semantics such as stem versus leaves. Current segmentation algorithms assume complete scene information captured by external sensors or do not explicitly enforce temporal consistency, making them unreliable during real-world deployment. In this work, we introduce a dataset containing annotated sequences of partial tomato plant point clouds and propose two learning-based algorithms for segmentation, rewarding consistent predictions along a trajectory. We first adapt the TomatoWUR dataset for robotics applications to include local point cloud observations simulated on the STEMbot. Our models take advantage of the temporal correlation between consecutive scans in a trajectory by augmenting a PTv3 model with batched sequence inputs and a long short term memory (LSTM) module. In addition, we propose a new metric to quantify the temporal consistency of inference along a trajectory by measuring the 'flicker' between predictions. Finally, we evaluate the performance and temporal consistency of our models and compare to the original baseline.

## Introduction

<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure1} alt="STEMbot navigating under a plant canopy. Under Submission iROS 2026, do not distribute." />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 1: STEMbot navigating under a plant canopy. Under Submission iROS 2026, do not distribute.</Fragment>

Organic agriculture is extremely labor intensive and difficult to automate [1]. Eighteen percent of yearly plant growth loss is atrributed to invasive pests. While weeds account for the highest component of annual plant growth loss (34\% of loss), mechanical removal and herbicides have been very effective in controlling their growth [2]. Pest management, on the other hand, remains a difficult problem for modern farmers. 

In non-organic farming, sythetic pesticides have been shown to improve crop productivity [2]. In organic farming, we seek to find non-invasive methods of pest management, to avoid the use of harmful chemicals for food production. Drone images have been used to detect pests through  slight wavelength changes in light reflecting off crops caused by plant stress. The detection of pest hotspots allowa farmers to precisely treat their crops with natural enemies or limited use of pesticides. While drone images can provide many insights, they struggle to identify individual pests such as the tobacco hornworm [3][4], which hide deep within plant canopies and are often occluded by leaves and other foliage [5]. 

Under-canopy plant climbing robots like STEMbot (Figure 1) offer an alternative solution to pest detection [6]. By traversing the stems of plants, STEMbot captures under-cannopy scans and detects camouflaged pests such as the the tobacco hornworm. For the motion planner to accurately generate climbing trajectories,  STEMbot must semantically understand the scene and differentiate which obstacles are climbable (the stem) and what needs to be avoided (the leaves). This process of quickly translating STEMbot visual input into tomato plant segmentation remains a critical challenge in STEMbot development. 

Building on the TomatoWUR dataset [7], which features full point clouds of tomato plants in the world frame, we generate two new datasets of static partial observations, and partial observations along a trajectory. Just like TomatoWUR, each point cloud is semantically labeled. Our baseline uses Point Transformer Version 3 (PTv3) for segmentation of the original full tomato point clouds. While the PTv3 model accurately segments singular tomato point clouds in the world frame, STEMbot follows trajectories with partial observations in sensor frame. Our key motivation is to adapt the PTv3 model to work with the STEMbot's visual input. Because the PTv3 model can only be applied to single frames, temporal inconsistencies (flickering) between consecutive predictions may occur. To address this limitation, we propose two new models: PTv3 with the addition of a Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) module, as well as trajectory-batched PTv3.

The main contributions of this paper are as follows:

  - A reproduction of the Point Cloud Transformer (PTv3) architecture for semantic segmentation of tomato plants.
  
  - Two new datasets of partial point cloud scans and trajectories generated using the STEMbot's camera and motion planner.
  
  - Two temporal extensions for the PTv3 algorithm.

  - A custom flickering evaluation metric to measure temporal inconsistencies in predictions along a trajectory.




## Related Work
This section introduces the background of existing tomato plant datasets, point cloud segmentation models, and temporal reasoning in machine learning.

### TomatoWUR Dataset
Van Marrewijk et. al. [7] introduce the TomatoWUR dataset for 3D plant phenotyping. The dataset includes  annotated 3D point cloud scans of 44 tomato plants, labeled by RGB color and class, along with annotated plant skeletons and ground truth measurements. This approach is acceptable in a laboratory setting where multi-view scans are possible, but breaks down in a practical setting where the robot only has access to a limited view of the plant restricted by its field of view and occlusions. Our work does not propose a novel dataset to replace TomatoWUR, but instead adapts TomatoWUR for robotic applications by extending its scope to include local partial point cloud scans and sequences of partial point clouds along simulated trajectories in both world and sensor frame.

### Point Cloud Segmentation
RGB-D cameras are an increasingly common sensor modality in robotics, capturing RGB images paired with pixel-level depth measurements. Even in cases where only a standard camera is available, foundation models such as DepthAnything v2 [8] and Dinov3 [9] are being used to infer depth from monocular images using internet-scale data. Thus, it is important to create semantic segmentation that works directly on 3D representations.

Deep-learning based 2D-to-3D methods [10] first perform 2D segmentation on a set of images, and subsequently backproject the 2D labels into 3D space. However, these methods require multiple images taken from known camera viewpoints, which is infeasible for robots in an agricultural setting.

Other deep-learning architectures directly use 3D point clouds as input, and output classification labels for every point. Liu et. al and Choy et. al extract local feautures using convolution with Point-voxel convolutional neural networks [11] and Minkowski convolutional neural networks [12]. Others design their own local features specific to point sets, such as in PointNet++ [13]. Transformer architectures Swin3D [14] and PointTransformer v3 (PTv3) [15] use multi-head attention between patches of points and have previously been shown to be successful at point cloud segmentation [16]. Van Marrewijk et. al. [17] evaluates a subset of the above methods on the TomatoWUR dataset. Results indicate that transformer-based models and 2D-to-3D segmentation perform comparably. Since our application involves a single dynamically moving camera, and we cannot guarantee perfect alignment of frames, we focus on improving transformer-based architectures, in particular PTv3 [18].


### Temporal Reasoning in Machine Learning
Many 3D segmentation methods focus on predicting labels for a single static point cloud, which works well for singular frames but is unreliable over a continuous sequence of inputs. Applying these methods directly may result in flickering, where semantic predictions of the same points frequently flip between different labels in consecutive frames. In contrast, an ideal segmentation model would incorporate the knowledge that points should retain their labels throughout a sequence to make consistent, and more accurate, predictions. Since the early days of deep learning, researchers have been developing methods of processing sequential data in a neural network. Long short term memory (LSTM) modules are similar to recurrent neural networks, except with memory blocks in the hidden layers [17]. LSTMs learn to remember and forget parameters which help the network manage its memory of previous inputs. Transformers are another natural way to handle sequential input [18][19], using multi-head attention to learn the correlation between various sequence elements. We explore applying both an LSTM module, and attention between point clouds in a trajectory, with PTv3 to extend the static tomato plant segmentation work of Van Marrewijk et. al. to a sequence of local views of the plant along continuous trajectories of plant climbing robots.

## Paper Reproduction
<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure2} alt="Pipeline Diagram for Point Transformer v3 (PTv3), taken directly from the paper" />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 2: Pipeline Diagram for Point Transformer v3 (PTv3), taken directly from the paper</Fragment>
</Figure>

Van Marrewijk et. al. [10] proposes and evaluates four different models for tomato plant segmentation-- 2D-to-3D segmentation, MinkUNet [11], Swin3D [12], and PTv3 [13]. We chose to focus on the most performant PTv3 model to reproduce and extend.

The core architecture of Point Transformer V3 (PTv3) consists of an initialization stage and sequential encoder blocks (Figure 2). Instead of treating point clouds as unordered sets, the initialization stage serializes a point cloud input by enforcing structure, allowing the attention mechanism of the transformer to capture different spatial relationships[14]. Specifically, PTv3 defines four serializations that order the points using different geometries: Z-Order, Trans Z-Order, Hilbert, and Trans-Hilbert. Each ordering is assigned a serialized embedding. As [15] does not focus on downstream prediction, the decoder is unspecified. For tomato plant segmentation, Van Marrewijk adds a decoder architecture identical to the encoder blocks in Figure 2, substituting the pooling layers for up-sampling layers [10].

We initialize the PTv3 model with pretrained weights and evaluate it on the TomatoWUR test set using the split defined by [10]. Figure 3 compares the mean and per-class IoU results reported in the original paper to our reproduction results. Our results remain within 1$\%$ of the values reported by [10], ensuring a fair baseline for our experiments in Section V.

## Extensions 

### Dataset Extension

The TomatoWUR dataset [7] provides high-fidelity, segmented 3D point clouds of tomato plants reconstructed from
15 surrounding camera viewpoints per plant. While valuable,
this setup is impractical for real-world deployment due to
the requirement of dense multi-view coverage. To address
this limitation, we extend the dataset in two directions: (1)
unordered partial scans that reflect incomplete observations,
and (2) temporally ordered partial scans generated from simulated STEMbot trajectories. For all extensions, we enforce
train/test splits at the plant level, ensuring that scans from
the same plant do not appear in both sets. Furthermore, each
dataset contains both world frame and sensor frame data
depending on the point of inference in the robot’s localization
pipeline.

#### Unordered Partial Scans:
<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure4} alt="Example partial point clouds from the partial point cloud dataset. Each point cloud is generated using the camera frustum of STEMbot (Intel RealSense D405), from a climbing location on the plant stem." />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 4: Example partial point clouds from the partial point cloud dataset. Each point cloud is generated using the camera frustum of STEMbot (Intel RealSense D405), from a climbing location on the plant stem.</Fragment>
</Figure>

For each tomato plant in the
TomatoWUR dataset, we generate 32 partial scans. To ensure
scan realism, we simulate the placement of the STEMbot
robot [6] on randomly selected stems with feasible sensor
poses. Each scan is acquired using a simulated d405 Intel
RealSense stereo depth camera, modeled via its corresponding camera frustum. Figure 4 shows two examples from our
data generation pipeline.
The variability in sensor placement results in a diverse
set of partial observations, with individual scans of up to
50,000 points. This formulation enables evaluation under
realistic sensing constraints, where perception must operate
on incomplete, viewpoint-limited observations rather than
full multi-view reconstructions.

#### Temporally Ordered Partial Scans:
<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure5} alt="An example trajectory from our partial point cloud trajectory dataset. Each trajectory has 8 waypoints, generated using the STEMbot motion planner with a specified start and goal state. Example trajectories include vertical climbs and descents, as well as switches onto branch junctions." />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 5: An example trajectory from our partial point cloud trajectory dataset. Each trajectory has 8 waypoints, generated using the STEMbot motion planner with a specified start and goal state. Example trajectories include vertical climbs and descents, as well as switches onto branch junctions.</Fragment>
</Figure>

We additionally
generate two STEMbot trajectories per tomato plant using the
motion planner developed in [6] for a total of 88 trajectories.
Each trajectory consists of eight temporally ordered partial
scans, captured along a feasible robot path. This temporal
structure enables evaluation under sequential sensing conditions, where observations are correlated through robot motion
rather than independently sampled viewpoints. Figure 5
shows an example trajectory and the camera frustums used
to collect the partial point cloud scan information.


### Algorithmic Extension

In this section, we describe two different methods to
incorporate temporal knowledge in the segmentation model
for point cloud sequences: (1) a recurrent model based on
long short-term memory (LSTM) and (2) batched sequences
with attention.

#### Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM):
<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure6} alt="Visualization of the PTv3 LSTM architecture." />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 6: Visualization of the PTv3 LSTM architecture.</Fragment>
</Figure>

To mitigate temporal inconsistency (e.g. segmentation flickering), the model
requires information from prior frames in the sequence. We
introduce an LSTM module to model temporal dependencies
across the trajectory. As shown in Figure 6A, the LSTM
is placed at the network bottleneck between the encoder
and decoder, allowing it to operate on a compact latent
representation.

At each timestep, the encoder embedding is passed
through the LSTM allowing the hidden state to aggregate
information across frames. The LSTM output is then fused
with the raw encoder embedding via an additive skip connection before being passed into the decoder.

#### Batched Sequence Attention:
Our second approach
takes advantage of the transformer architecture through
multi-head attention to capture temporal artifacts between
partial point clouds in a trajectory. This method, shown
in Figure 6B, feeds all 8 partial point clouds from each
waypoint in the trajectory to the transformer. Multi-head
attention allows for inherent sequence information between
frames.

  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 7: Mean IoU comparison between a model trained on the full point
cloud dataset and the partial point cloud dataset, tested on both full

</Figure>

We evaluate two PTv3 models following the architecture
in [10]: one trained on full point clouds (FPC) and one
trained on our partial point cloud (PPC) dataset. As shown
in Figure 7, the FPC-trained model exhibits a substantial
performance drop when evaluated on partial observations,
indicating limited generalization to incomplete viewpoints.
In contrast, the PPC-trained model achieves significantly
higher performance on partial point clouds while maintaining
comparable performance on full point clouds. Our results
show that the original model, trained on full point clouds,
overfits and cannot generalize to partial scans; conversely,
our new model, trained on partial scans, generalizes well to
full point clouds.

### Frame of Reference Analysis

<Figure>
  <Picture slot="figure" src={Figure9} alt="oU comparison between world-frame and sensor-frame representa-
tions for a PTv3 model trained on partial point clouds. All other training
and evaluation conditions are held constant." />
  <Fragment slot="caption">Figure 9: IoU comparison between world-frame and sensor-frame representations for a PTv3 model trained on partial point clouds. All other training
and evaluation conditions are held constant.</Fragment>
</Figure>

We evaluate a PTv3 model trained on partial point clouds
(PPC) using inputs expressed in either the world frame
or the sensor (camera) frame. As shown in Figure 9, we
observe negligible performance differences between the two
representations, indicating that the model is largely invariant
to the choice of input coordinate frame. Intuitively, this is not
the result that we expected, specifically for the ”main stem”
and ”side stem” categories. In world frame, the main stem
semantic class is always in a nearly identical pose. For the
sensor frame scans, side stem or main stem could be in any
configuration depending on the pose of STEMbot. Despite
this challenge, our models achieved nearly the same mean
and per-class IoUs.

**Table I:** Mean IoU, Accuracy, and Flicker Rate of Baseline PTv3 Model,
Batched PTv3 Model, and Recurrent LSTM Model Across All Trajectories


For temporal consistency, we evaluate vanilla PTv3 trained
on PPC, Recurrent PTv3 with incorporated LSTM, and
batched sequence attention PTv3. We classify each frame
of the trajectory test set sequentially. To calculate temporal consistency, we calculate the number of overlapping
points between each frame and quantify flicker rate as (Flicker Rate = # Reclassified Points / # Overlapping Points)

As shown in Table I, we find
that vanilla PTv3 has a modest average flicker rate of 3.38%
with both recurrent PTv3 and batched sequence attention
PTv3 showing reduced temporal flicker. Batched sequence
attention PTv3 has the highest temporal consistency with an
average flicker rate of just 2.96%. In addition, a visualization
of the areas of flicker was developed for each plant. Figure
8 shows point clouds generated by overlaying partial point
clouds from a trajectory on three plants in the dataset. Points
that had predictions change as the trajectory point clouds
were fed into the model are highlighted in cyan. From this
visual, it is evident that flickering most occurs at boundaries
between distinct areas of the plant (e.g. where a side stem
extrudes from the main stem). The difference in model did
not change the location of where flickering occurred.

## Conclusions

We find that PTv3 trained on PPC significantly improves
segmentation performance on the held out PPC test set
compared to FPC training. We further note that PPC trained
PTv3 maintains equivalent performance to the FPC trained
model on the FPC test set (see Figure 7). This indicates that
privileged FPC information is not necessary for a reliable
point cloud segmentation model, opening the door for more
realistic robot applications.

Our experiments also showed negligible performance differences between models trained and evaluated on point
clouds in world frame vs sensor frame. The simplest method
of gathering point clouds is to convert raw sensor frame
RGBD images into a point cloud. Transforming raw sensor
frame point clouds into world frame adds additional implementation complexity and a potential source of error. Our
results suggest that equivalent performance is possible with
sensor frame point clouds, decoupling segmentation from
global mapping.

Finally, we consider the ”flicker” or reclassification of
points between consecutive frames in a trajectory. Our results
suggest that both the incorporation of an LSTM and batched
sequential inference improve temporal consistency in point
cloud segmentation across trajectories. The reduce in flicker
can improve robot certainty as fewer sections of the observed
point cloud are reclassified from frame to frame. However,
we note that while flicker rate decreases for both of our
temporal extensions, accuracy and IoU do not significantly
increase from baseline (see Table I). This is somewhat
unexpected as the sole training objective is segmentation
accuracy. Therefore, we would expect a decrease in flicker
rate to correspond to an increase in IoU/accuracy. The lack
of improvement in accuracy suggests that our temporal
extensions may be more consistent both when correct and
incorrect, indicating a need for further study.
